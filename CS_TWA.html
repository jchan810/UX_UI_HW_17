Teach with Africa

I served as UX Designer with a strong focus on information architecture, hierarchy on individual pages, and visual elements.

View

Project Overview:

Teach with Africa is a non-profit organization, known primarily for their exchange programs for American and South African educators. Their goal is to provide South African children quality education, as the country is in desperate need of trained educators, and only 5% of Black South African students qualify for a university application.

Role:

UX Designer

Tools:

Figma, Miro, Trello, Google Suite

Team:

UC Berkeley Extension group and organization founders, Marjorie & Larry Schlenoff

Timline:

2 weeks, 2020

Problem

The organization’s original website was cluttered, lacked information hierarchy, and made finding important information extremely difficult, if not impossible, for potential users. As a result, the organization was not receiving enough applicants for their exchange programs. Founder, Marjorie Schlenoff, wanted a complete website redesign to improve website navigation, visual aesthetics, and increase engagement with Teach with Africa.

Contraints

While we essentially had free range with our redesign, we were given a couple crucial constrains. When choosing a color palette, we were limited to variations of the South African flag, as it had to match the Teach with Africa logo, and Marjorie requested for the “Gallery” page to remain on the dropdown navigation, rather than being moved to the website footer.

Solution

We aimed to create a site that is easily navigable with images, colors, and other visual design elements that align with Teach with Africa’s core messaging. Creating an enjoyable experience will encourage users to interact with the organization, and provide clear paths for program enrollment and donations.

Strategy

My group and I caught the organization at the beginning of their own redesign process, and worked directly with founder, Marjorie Schlenoff, to discuss the organization’s mission and what elements of the website were most important to Teach with Africa. We started by defining our user and researching various study abroad and exchange programs that had a similar informational structure to what we were striving towards, which helped us reorganize the hierarchy of the website’s content.
